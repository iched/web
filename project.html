Background

Antibiotic resistance represents a significant and growing threat to global public health[1]. Current strategies for antibiotic modification suffer from limitations such as limited universality and single mechanistic actions[2]. A well-known mechanism for the development of resistance to multiple antibiotics involves a decrease in microbial redox levels, which leads to a dormant state and a reduction in the uptake of antibiotics. Aditionally, the robust polymer matrix of biofilms further shields microorganisms by restricting antibiotic penetration. Previous study has reported that disruption of redox homeostasis can effectively eliminate the "dormant bodies" of microorganisms and disrupt the integrity of biofilms[3]. Moreover, nanozymes exhibit enzyme-mimetic catalytic activity and modulate microbial redox homeostasis through the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS)[4-6]. Therefore, we can modulate the intracellular redox homeostasis of microorganisms to enhance their uptake of antibiotics by designing antibiotic nanozymes.

Problem Statement

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) occurs when bacteria undergo changes that reduce the effectiveness of therapeutic agents used to treat infections, and has emerged as one of the major public health threats of the 21st century. According to the Review on Antimicrobial Resistance commissioned by the UK government, AMR could cause up to 10 million deaths annually by 2050, with associated economic costs soaring to $100 trillion[7].A statistical analysis of data[1] from 204 countries and territories worldwide in 2019, covering 23 pathogens and 88 pathogen-drug combinations, revealed that an estimated 4.95 million (95% UI 3.62--6.57) deaths were associated with resistant bacterial infections across these 88 pathogen-drug pairs. Among these, 1.27 million (0.91--1.71) deaths were directly attributable to antimicrobial resistance. When compared to all underlying causes of death globally in 2019, had these infections been non-resistant, AMR would rank as the third leading cause of death at the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Level 3, surpassed only by ischemic heart disease and stroke. These findings underscore that bacterial antimicrobial resistance has become a major global health challenge, highlighting the critical importance of developing novel antibacterial agents and reversing drug resistance.

Challenges

The current landscape of antimicrobial resistance is highly complex and dynamically evolving. Among the various resistance mechanisms, reduced membrane permeability---leading to diminished intracellular antibiotic accumulation---represents one of the most critical and widespread strategies employed by bacteria. In response to key resistance determinants such as diminished membrane permeability, overexpression of efflux pumps, and enzymatic antibiotic inactivation, researchers have developed several innovative antibiotic modification strategies. Notable approaches include the design of novel β-lactamase inhibitors, the development of siderophore-antibiotic conjugates, antibody-antibiotic conjugates, and combination therapies with proton pump inhibitors.While current strategies for antibiotic modification demonstrate promising potential for clinical application and can effectively counter certain aspects of antimicrobial resistance, several critical limitations remain: reliance on membrane protein-mediated transport still carries inherent risks of resistance development; high production costs and limited generalizability (e.g., structural modification of antibiotic molecules or antibody conjugation) hinder broad implementation; and approaches relying solely on a single mechanism to reverse bacterial resistance often exhibit limited efficacy.Therefore, our team endeavors to construct antibiotic-nanozyme conjugates that employ multimodal antibacterial mechanisms. This strategy is designed not only to reverse existing antibiotic resistance but also to prevent the induction of further resistance mutations, thereby offering a novel therapeutic approach to combat antimicrobial resistance.

Solutions

Nanozymes represent a class of nanomaterials with significant potential and promising application prospects.We propose the rational design of a nanozyme capable of disrupting bacterial resistance mechanisms. This approach aims to reverse antibiotic resistance, thereby resensitizing bacteria to conventional antibiotics and enhancing their efficacy.

A critical component of this strategy is the selection of an appropriate antibiotic agent. Our focus is directed towards hemin, an iron-containing porphyrin. Hemin serves as a cofactor for various enzymatic reactions in vivo and facilitates the transport of gaseous molecules. Its catalytic activity is primarily governed by histidine-modulated axial coordination, with its inherent porphyrin structure constituting the foundation for its enzyme-mimetic properties [10, 11].

Many antibiotic molecules possess functional groups capable of effectively displacing the chloride atom in ferric hemin (Fe(III)-hemin). These groups can act as axial ligands to the iron-N4 plane, forming a pentacoordinate structural unit [12].

This unit can subsequently self-assemble into nanoparticle aggregates via various non-covalent interactions, including hydrogen bonding, salt bridges, π-π stacking, and cation-π interactions, resulting in the formation of an antibiotic-nanozyme complex [13].

This engineered antibiotic-nanozyme retains its constituent antibiotic component. Crucially, it acquires catalytic properties through the axial ligand substitution, mimicking the natural histidine coordination found in many peroxidases. This catalytic capability enables the nanozyme to effectively degrade bacterial biofilms and alter membrane permeability. The disruption of the biofilm matrix and enhanced membrane permeability collectively facilitate improved antibiotic uptake by the target bacteria[14]. Furthermore, the molecular self-assembly synthesis of these antibiotic-nanozymes offers a straightforward and scalable production methodology, which is advantageous for potential future translation.

The proposed design strategy for antibiotic-nanozymes is likely applicable to a broad spectrum of antibiotic classes, potentially offering a significant improvement in addressing the current crisis of antibiotic resistance. We further postulate that selecting different cofactor molecules (adjuvants) beyond hemin could expand the applicability and provide greater multifunctionality to this platform.

Feasibility

Our preliminary assessments of the antimicrobial activity of various synthesized antibiotic nanozymes have demonstrated that these constructs not only enhance the efficacy of conventional antibiotics by effectively combating both planktonic cells and biofilms of drug-resistant bacteria, but also employ multimodal mechanisms to reverse resistance patterns. These findings confirm the feasibility and rational design of our research project.

Our team members exhibit a strong collaborative spirit, with each individual contributing unique expertise, enabling efficient task completion through well-defined division of labor. When encountering challenges, open communication and mutual support among team members have been instrumental in overcoming obstacles. By systematically summarizing lessons learned from both successful and unsuccessful experiments, we continuously identify areas for improvement and refine our research direction. Furthermore, our advisor provides exceptional guidance and timely assistance whenever difficulties arise. Additionally, sufficient laboratory funding ensures the sustained progress of our experimental work.
