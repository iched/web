Where Does Our Inspiration Come From?

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) stands as one of the most critical challenges to global health today and is a leading cause of antibiotic treatment failure. When confronted with antibiotic therapy, the robust polymeric matrix of bacterial biofilms often restricts antibiotic penetration.[1] Furthermore, persister cells within biofilms typically cease proliferation and reduce metabolic activity to conserve energy, thereby ensuring their survival.[2] Additionally, bacteria increase the production of hydrogen sulfide gas in response to stress and damage, which helps them combat oxidative stress and enhances their antibiotic tolerance. [3]

Nanozymes are a class of artificial nanomaterials possessing catalytic functions akin to natural enzymes.[4] They can directly attack bacterial structures such as cell membranes and DNA through mechanisms like catalyzing the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS).[6][7] More uniquely, their multi-mechanistic physicochemical bactericidal approach is less likely to induce bacterial resistance, offering a promising new direction to address the dilemma of antibiotic resistance.

Consequently, our team aims to enhance antibiotic uptake by modulating the bacterial redox homeostasis. We are developing antibiotic-nanozymes that synergize with antibiotics and inhibit biofilm formation, thereby reversing antibiotic resistance and improving the efficacy of bacterial eradication.

How Do We Design Antibiotic-Nanozymes?

We have successfully designed antibiotic-nanozymes constructed via axial coordination and supramolecular self-assembly, utilizing a range of antibiotics including Gentamicin and Fluconazole, along with hemin. In this system, the antibiotic acts as a structural scaffold, enabling targeting of intracellular sites and exerting its inherent antimicrobial mechanism. Simultaneously, hemin serves as the catalytic center, efficiently generating ROS to disrupt bacterial biofilms and their antioxidant defense systems. The synergistic integration of these two components forms a functionally unified composite material, achieving a powerful antibacterial effect where "1+1 >> 2," effectively overcoming the resistance issues associated with traditional antibiotics.

Selection of the Catalytic Core (Hemin)

Structural Advantage:

Hemin is the active center of natural peroxidases. Its central iron ion provides two axial coordination sites. In natural enzymes, one site coordinates with histidine to stabilize the structure, while the other participates in the catalytic reaction. This study leverages this characteristic by using antibiotic molecules to replace the protein scaffold and the role of histidine in natural enzymes, allowing them to axially coordinate with hemin and spontaneously self-assemble into nanozyme structures with enzyme-like activity.

Biosafety Advantage:

As an endogenous substance in the human body (e.g., the core of hemoglobin), hemin is metabolized through established pathways, thus possessing inherent biocompatibility and degradability,[9] avoiding the potential long-term toxicity risks associated with some nanomaterials.

Antibiotic Selection:

This study selected a series of representative antibiotics, including Gentamicin (GM), Fluconazole (FCZ), Ceftriaxone (CRO), and Rapamycin (RAPA).These four drugs include antibacterial agents (GM, CRO) and an antifungal agent (FCZ).They cover three classic bactericidal pathways: inhibition of cell wall synthesis (CRO), inhibition of protein synthesis (GM), and disruption of cell membrane integrity (FCZ);They represent different chemical structure families: aminoglycosides, azoles, β-lactams, and macrolides, respectively.

All these antibiotics can assemble with hemin via axial coordination and co-polymerize to form antibiotic-nanozymes, demonstrating the universality of our research approach for antibiotics with diverse chemical structures.

Virtual Screening:

We employed computational virtual screening to predict and rank the binding affinity of dozens of antibiotics with hemin. The top two antibiotics with the highest predicted affinity were selected for further assembly with hemin into antibiotic-nanozymes, followed by experimental validation. The experimental results showed that nanozymes constructed from antibiotics with higher binding affinity exhibited stronger peroxidase (POD)-like activity and bactericidal efficacy. Computational virtual screening enables the rapid and accurate prediction of antibiotics with high binding affinity to hemin, facilitating the construction of high-performance nanozymes, saving time and economic costs, and significantly shortening the research and development cycle.

Co-assembly:

Various antibiotics (e.g., GM, FCZ, CRO) contain electron-donating atoms such as nitrogen and oxygen. These atoms can provide lone pair electrons to form stable coordination bonds with the axial empty orbitals of the iron ion in hemin, displacing the originally bound chloride ion and forming an antibiotic-hemin complex.

Supramolecular Self-Assembly:

A single coordination complex is insufficient to constitute a functional nanozyme. Further ordered self-assembly via supramolecular interactions is necessary to form stable structures with dimensions ranging from tens to hundreds of nanometers. This process is primarily driven by three intermolecular forces:

1. Strong π-π stacking interactions exist between the porphyrin ring of hemin and the aromatic structures of the antibiotics.

2. The hydrophobic regions of both components spontaneously aggregate in the aqueous environment to minimize interfacial energy.

3. Where spatially permissible, one hemin center can simultaneously coordinate with two antibiotic molecules, forming a stable "sandwich"-type 1:2 complex, significantly enhancing the stability and catalytic efficiency of the assembly.

Validation of Universality:

This strategy is not dependent on any specific antibiotic. We have validated its effectiveness across multiple structural classes of antibiotics (including GM, FCZ, CRO, and even RAPA), demonstrating the broad applicability of this platform technology.

Functional Mechanism: Multi-mechanistic Synergy to Reverse Resistance

The antibiotic-hemin nanozymes developed in this study combat drug-resistant bacteria through synergistic multi-mechanisms. Their core function relies on their peroxidase-like activity, catalyzing the conversion of endogenous H₂O₂ into highly toxic ·OH radicals, which disrupts the bacterial redox homeostasis and consequently reverses resistance. Simultaneously, they degrade the biofilm matrix and eradicate persister cells. Furthermore, this system can induce fungal ferroptosis, inhibit efflux pumps, and suppress the H₂S-based antioxidant defense system, comprehensively enhancing the bactericidal effect. This bioinspired synergistic strategy provides an efficient new approach to overcoming antibiotic resistance, holding broad prospects for clinical application.
