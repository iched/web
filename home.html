Antibiotic Nanozyme

A novel approach to combat antimicrobial resistance through molecular co-assembly

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) Challenge

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) stands as one of the most pressing global health challenges of our time, responsible for at least 1 million deaths annually worldwide [1], a figure projected to rise steadily. Scientific investigations reveal that this crisis stems not only from the spread of resistant bacteria but also involves intricate underlying mechanisms: on one hand, bacteria rapidly share resistance genes via plasmids, where a handful of "superplasmids" can confer resistance to multiple first-line and even last-resort antibiotics [2]; on the other hand, surprisingly, some pathogens may even enhance their virulence by shedding certain resistance mechanisms, potentially increasing patient mortality [3]. Compounding this problem, nearly three decades have passed since the 1990s without the introduction of a truly novel class of antibiotics into clinical practice, leaving our antimicrobial arsenal critically depleted. Therefore, developing innovative strategies capable of breaking the current resistance deadlock has become an urgent imperative.

Nanozymes in AMR Combat

In the ongoing battle against antimicrobial resistance, nanozymes—a class of artificial nanomaterials endowed with natural enzyme-like catalytic activities—are emerging as a promising frontier with transformative potential[4]. These nanozymes can generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) and other antibacterial agents through catalytic reactions, directly damaging bacterial cell membranes, DNA, and other vital structures, thereby effectively eliminating a range of drug-resistant pathogens, including methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) [5] [6]. More uniquely, their multi-mechanistic physicochemical bactericidal mode makes it difficult for bacteria to develop resistance, offering a new direction to overcome the current AMR crisis.

Furthermore, nanozymes possess the ability to effectively penetrate and disrupt stubborn bacterial biofilms [6], a property critical for treating chronic and persistent infections. It has been demonstrated that when nanozymes are immobilized on medical dressings, they can eliminate over 90% of Candida albicans within just 10 minutes [4], underscoring their highly efficient antimicrobial activity and practical application potential. As such, nanozymes are regarded as a powerful tool in confronting the challenges of the "post-antibiotic era"[6] .

Novel Antibiotic Nanozyme Design

Here, we designed a novel class of antibiotic nanozymes through the molecular co-assembly of antibiotics with hemin chloride to combat antimicrobial resistance.[9]

The synthesis of these antibiotic nanozymes demonstrates remarkable versatility. We selected a series of representative antibiotics, including Gentamicin (GM), Fluconazole (FCZ), and virtually screened molecules such as Rapamycin (RAPA) and Ceftriaxone. Each of these antibiotics successfully co-assembled with hemin chloride via axial coordination and copolymerization, forming stable antibiotic nanozyme.

Fig. 3. Schematic diagram of the assembly of various antibiotics and hemin into antibiotic nanozymes

Multifaceted Antibacterial Mechanisms

The antibiotic nanozymes exert potent bactericidal effects through the following multifaceted mechanisms[10]:

  1. The antibiotic nanozymes effectively degrade bacterial biofilms.[11]
  2. Possessing peroxidase (POD)-like activity, the antibiotic nanozymes induce oxidative damage to bacteria.[12]
  3. The antibiotic nanozymes function as ferroptosis inducers, triggering bacterial ferroptosis.
  4. Within bacterial cells, the nanozymes release the constituent antibiotics, achieving synergistic antibacterial action.
